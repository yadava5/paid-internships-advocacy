Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

The Foundation of Worker Rights Since 1938

What is the FLSA?

The Fair Labor Standards Act is the primary federal law governing minimum wage, overtime pay, recordkeeping, and youth employment. It establishes that for-profit employers must pay employees for their workâ€”including interns who qualify as employees.

Whether you're entitled to pay depends on the "primary beneficiary test", which examines who benefits most from the internship relationship.

Establishes federal minimum wage ($7.25/hour)
Requires overtime pay (1.5x for 40+ hours)
Applies to for-profit private sector employers

The 7-Factor Primary Beneficiary Test

How Courts Determine If You Should Be Paid

Since the landmark Glatt v. Fox Searchlight Pictures case (2016), courts use this flexible test to determine whether an intern is actually an employee entitled to wages.

1

No Expectation of Compensation

Both parties clearly understand there is no promise of pay. Any implied compensation suggests employment.

2

Educational Training

The internship provides training similar to an educational environment, including hands-on learning.

3

Tied to Formal Education

The internship is connected to the intern's academic program through coursework or academic credit.

4

Accommodates Academic Schedule

The internship corresponds to the academic calendar and works around class commitments.

5

Limited Duration

The internship lasts only as long as it provides beneficial learningâ€”not indefinitely.

6

Complements, Not Displaces

The intern's work supplements paid employees rather than replacing them, with significant educational value.

7

No Job Entitlement

Both parties understand the internship doesn't guarantee a paid position afterwards.

Important: No single factor is decisive. Courts weigh all circumstances together. If you're doing the same work as paid employees, you're likely entitled to compensation.

Landmark Court Cases

Rulings That Changed Intern Rights

2016

Glatt v. Fox Searchlight Pictures

U.S. Court of Appeals, 2nd Circuit

Interns on the film "Black Swan" sued for unpaid work. The court established the 7-factor primary beneficiary test, replacing the rigid 6-factor DOL test. This became the standard nationwide.

Read Full Opinion
2015

Schumann v. Collier Anesthesia

U.S. Court of Appeals, 11th Circuit

This case reinforced that courts must take a "holistic" approach to determining intern status, considering the totality of circumstances rather than rigid criteria.

Read Full Opinion
1947

Walling v. Portland Terminal Co.

U.S. Supreme Court

The foundational case that established trainees are not employees when the employer receives no immediate advantage from their work. Still cited in modern rulings.

Read Full Opinion

State-by-State Protections

Some States Go Beyond Federal Law

While the FLSA sets the federal baseline, many states have enacted stronger protections for interns. Here are key state-level developments:

California Stricter

California's Division of Labor Standards Enforcement (DLSE) applies an 11-factor test that's more protective than the federal standard. The state also has strong anti-retaliation protections.

  • Minimum wage: $16.00/hour (2024)
  • Stricter classification standards
  • Strong enforcement mechanisms
California DLSE Guidelines
New York Stricter

New York uses criteria similar to federal standards but with additional anti-discrimination protections that specifically cover unpaid interns (since 2014).

  • Minimum wage: $15.00-$16.00/hour (varies by region)
  • Interns protected under Human Rights Law
  • Harassment protections for interns
NY Human Rights Law
Oregon Stricter

Oregon expanded intern protections in 2014, treating interns as employees for purposes of anti-discrimination law, regardless of compensation status.

  • Minimum wage: $14.70/hour (2024)
  • Full discrimination protections
  • Civil rights coverage for interns
Oregon BOLI Guidelines
Illinois Stricter

The Illinois Human Rights Act was amended to protect unpaid interns from sexual harassment and discrimination in 2014.

  • Minimum wage: $14.00/hour (2024)
  • Anti-harassment for all interns
  • Discrimination protections
Illinois DHR
Tip: Always check your state's specific lawsâ€”you may have more protections than federal law provides. Contact your state's Department of Labor for guidance.

The Non-Profit Exception

Different Rules for Charitable Organizations

When Unpaid Work May Be Legal

The FLSA recognizes an exception for individuals who volunteer their time freely and without expectation of compensation for religious, charitable, civic, or humanitarian purposes at non-profit organizations.

However, this exception has limits. Even at non-profits, if your work looks like employment, you may still be entitled to pay.

What To Do If You're Unpaid

Taking Action on Your Rights

1

Document Everything

Keep records of your hours, tasks, communications, and any promises made about compensation or job opportunities.

2

Evaluate the 7 Factors

Review the primary beneficiary test. If your work primarily benefits the employer, you may be entitled to pay.

3

Contact Your School

If the internship is for credit, talk to your career center. They can help mediate and clarify expectations.

4

File a Complaint

Contact the Department of Labor's Wage and Hour Division to file a complaint. They investigate violations.
